    • The current state of Indonesia

    Over 270 million people in Indonesia use energy every day. That’s why it’s important to make sure that the energy is produced cleanly. Currently, Indonesia doesn’t do so well. In 2022, Indonesia used a total of 25328 TW. This is a lot of energy, unfortunately, only 7% of this energy was produced by renewable means. So what is holding Indonesia back from producing clean energy?

    Let's find out in the next few dimensions.

    Spatial dimensions

    Indonesia is made up of three main islands. Java, Sumatra and Borneo island. Out of these three, Sumatra is the most densely populated. Java and Borneo are both densely forested and don’t have a big population. Borneo Island is mostly flat, the northern side of the island is mountainous. Java Island is flat on the east and mountainous on the west. Sumatra island is mostly mountainous with some spots of flat land. The land on Borneo Island is mostly used for palm oil production. The land on Java is mostly used for agriculture such as soybeans and rice. The land in Sumatra has most of the big cities. This is where most of the business is done. But there is also some agriculture being done on the island.

    Socio-cultural dimensions

    Indonesian culture is focused on the community, with a hierarchical structure. Indonesians believe in the concept of going royong (mutual assistance) and mufakat (consensus), and the national motto is Bhinneka Tunggal Ika (Unity in diversity). The population of Indonesia is around 300 million people. The birth rate is 16.817 per 1000 people and the death rate is 10.060. The growth rate is 1.63 per cent. Migration is not very big in Indonesia.

    Around 40.12 per cent of Indonesians aged 15 years and above had completed senior high school or more. Compulsory education in Indonesia begins at age seven and lasts a total of nine years. During this time, public schooling is offered at no cost. A minimum of one year of Indonesian language study is required if a child is to graduate from high school.

    To have a more sustainable country more people should go to school.

    Political dimensions

    Indonesia is a democratic country and its capitalistic. The president is Joko Widodo. He is part of the Indonesian democratic party of Struggle.

    Indonesia has trade pacts with Japan, Australia, South Korea, New Zealand, Chile, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Egypt, Iran, Malaysia, Nigeria, Turkey, Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, Switzerland, Mozambique, China and India.

    Indonesia has political pacts.

    Indonesia has a civil law system based on the Roman-Dutch model.

    Economical dimensions

    The GDP of Indonesia is $1.39 trillion (nominal; 2023 est.) and $4.39 trillion (PPP; 2023 est.) Indonesia has a large and in many cases unprospected, variety of mineral deposits. Mining, including the extraction of oil and natural gas, accounts for roughly one-tenth of the country's GDP, and through exports and taxation, it contributes substantially to foreign-exchange earnings and development.

    Explanation

    Indonesia generates energy mostly from coal and oil. Over 60% of all its energy is generated this way. Producing energy with geothermal central is way better than generating energy with coal and oil. The earth will always be geothermally active for as long as we will exist. That is not the case with coal or oil, both of those are finite. Another reason why geothermal energy is superior is that it doesn’t emit any greenhouse gasses during its production. This, of course, isn’t the case with coal and oil. Geothermal uses the heat of deep in the earth to turn water into steam and make it into steam. where you can have the steam drive turbines that generate electricity. Solar power is also a good solution for Java and Borneo. Because Borneo exports all kinds of natural resources. It is quite rich. Java is the richest island of them all having the capital city and a great touristic place. The best place to put solar panels is to put them in lakes or in cutdown forests and use that surplus of wood for the biomass industry. Indonesia is already a big exporter of biomass materials to other countries. But cutting down huge chunks of forests is a bad idea. Indonesia already started experimenting with solar parks on lakes, so this can be something to do again. On a bigger scale. Solar panels have cells in them that can convert sunlight into energy Hydropower works at the end of the rivers in lower laying areas. Placing a dam and generating electricity and having more control over the water. It can be dangerous to go tampering with the rivers because many people rely on them for fishing and building dams could be a disaster. There is a turbine located in the dam that gets turned around by the flowing water and a dynamo turning the motion of the turbine into energy.

    Costs

    Indonesia used 291 Terawatt hours in 2021. Java uses 67% of the total energy. That makes 291*0.63=194.97 Terawatt hours. On average, a geothermal power plant generates 100GWh per year. This means that we will need 1950 geothermal power plants to power all of Java. This will cost 487.5 billion dollars. Of course, isn’t practical or economically beneficial. That’s why we need to balance the geothermal power plants with solar power plants. On average, a solar power plant can produce 1.8 mWh per m2 per year. A reasonably sized solar park is around 15000m2. This will make our solar power plant produce 30 GWh per year. One square meter of solar panels costs around 10 dollars. This means that one entire park will cost 150 million dollars. Java will need 6500 solar power plants to power the entirety of Java. This will cost 975 billion dollars. Of course assumes, this that all of Java will run on green energy. However, this change is almost impossible to make within a reasonable amount of time. That’s why I think that it’s best if Java increases its renewable energy to 30%. This change will cost Java 130 billion dollars. Sumatra consumes 15% of Indonesia’s total energy which makes the island’s power consumption in 2021 43.65 TWh. We have already calculated that one geothermal power plant generates 100GWh per year and costs 250 million dollars. This means that we will need 437 power plants to generate all the energy for Sumatra. This will cost 110 billion dollars. We can also generate energy with hydropower. One plant generates 4 TWh and costs 5 million dollars. This means that it will take 11 hydropower plants to power all of Sumatra. This will cost only 55 million dollars. In order not to be fully dependent on hydropower, we still think it’s a good idea to install some geothermal power plants as well. That is why we will only install 10 hydropower plants. This will cost 50 million dollars and will generate 40Twh of energy. Meanwhile, we will have to install 37 geothermal plants. This will cost 9.5 billion dollars. This means that with just 9555 million dollars, Sumatra will only be using renewable energy. Borneo consumes 11% of Indonesia’s total energy which makes the island’s power consumption in 2021 32.01 TWh. Borneo Island will use solar parks and hydropower plants to generate its energy. One hydropower plant costs 5 million dollars and produces 4 TWh. One solar park costs 150 million dollars and produces 30 GWh on average. If we install 8 hydropower plants on the island, it will cost us 40 million dollars to power almost the entire island. The remaining energy needed can be generated with solar parks. This will cost us 14 million dollars, so in total 54 million dollars to get the entire island onto renewable energy.

    Conclusion

    The best energy source to use in Indonesia differs per island. In Java, it is a good idea to use geothermal energy combined with solar energy. We will be able to use geothermal energy as the island’s geothermal energy is easy to access. This is because of the number of volcanoes on the island. One downside of geothermal power plants is that they are pricy and hard to install. This is why it’s a good idea to combine power plants with solar power. On the eastern side of the island, there is a lot of potential for solar energy. Installing a solar park will be easy and will cost less than geothermal power plants. The energy retrieved from this park will be enough to supply power to eastern Java and Bali. Moving on to Sumatra. Sumatra is less populated and therefore requires less energy to be produced. As for the options for Java, there aren’t many. Biomass will be able to generate a lot of energy. This is a good option because Sumatra is densely forested. On the other hand, biomass is not a very green solution. Sumatra’s jungle has already been exploited for years for its trees to generate biomass energy. That’s why we believe that geothermal and hydropower are better alternatives. Both of these are possible as Sumatra is geothermally active and has rivers running through it which can be used to generate energy with hydropower stations. Lastly, we have Borneo Borneo island is the least populated of the three that we will discuss. Despite its population density, there are still some big cities on the island. The best option for these cities is hydropower and solar power. Biomass won’t be considered because of the same reason as discussed with Sumatra, it harms the environment. Hydropower is a good option as there are a lot of rivers that flow through the island. Although building a dam to generate this power is expensive. The other option is to build solar parks. These are way easier to build than hydropower centrals. They also cost less. The only problem with these is that a lot of space has to be cleared out for the panels. This could lead to further deforestation. So we now have discussed the best means of generating energy on the three main islands. For Java it is geothermal and solar power, for Sumatra it is geothermal energy and hydropower. And finally, for Borneo, it is Hydropower and solar power.
